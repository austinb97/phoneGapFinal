About

Here you can learn more about the reasoning and science that goes into horsepower measurements and altitude power loss. Keep in mind that there are many factors that go into these two measurements, and I have chosen to focus on the larger aspects.

        

Wheel Horsepower

        

When measuring a vehicle's power, there are two places to measure: at the crank, and at the wheels. Crank horsepower is measured at the engines crankshaft. this is directly affected by the engine and is therefore a good measure of the power output of an engine. It can be assumed that the same powertrain (engine, air filter, cooling system, exhaust, etc.) would produce the same crank horsepower in any other vehicle. Wheel horsepower, as the name implies, is measured at the wheels. Because transmission and drivetrain systems are not 100% efficient, some of the crank horsepower is lost. This is called drivetrain loss. Drivetrain loss is affected by the transmission and drive type. Automatic transmissions generally suffer more drivetrain loss than manuals. Rear wheel drive cars also experience more loss than front wheel drive, and all wheel drive loses the most power. There are some exceptions involving engine placement, for example the Porsche 911 is rear engine and rear wheel drive. This setup will have less drivetrain loss than a front engine rear wheel drive car. Car manufacturers will usually advertise the crank horsepower of a vehicle because the larger number sounds better, but if you measured the horsepower at the wheels it would not return the same number.

        

Altitude Loss

        

Internal combustion engines burn fuel and air to produce power. Generally speaking, the more air and fuel you cram into an engine, the more power it will make. However, at higher elevations the atmospheric pressure is lower and the air is thinner, resulting in less air being used by the engine and less power being made. The higher you go in elevation, the thinner the air gets. This is called altitude loss. This can be compensated for by using forced-induction systems like turbochargers or superchargers. Turbochargers and superchargers are different methods of doing the same thing, which is compressing air before it is fed into the engine. Force feeding air into the engine increases horsepower by giving it more to burn. Because forced-induction systems read the PSI of the air going through them they can account for the lower atmospheric pressure at elevation. This is why at higher elevations cars with smaller, turbocharged, four cylinder engines can keep up with or pass cars with larger naturally aspirated engines that would be faster at sea level. This is still, of course, entirely dependent on the horsepower difference between the two.
